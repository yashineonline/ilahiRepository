History:
Salaat-o-Salaam by Safee Siddiqi
Wafaat: Thursday 9 Muharram 1434

Notes about the translation
1 The poet hints that due to the barakat [blessings; grace] in Madina, the pleasant weather is so enchanting that even Janna [Paradise] itself would wish for that. On a subtle note, the writer is hinting that Madina is better than Janna. The poet could be using the weather as a metaphor to represent Huzoor Akram Risaalat (the Prophet) Sallallahu alayhi wassalam (SAW)

2
the four caliphs of Islam, peace be upon them,

3 moon may be referring to the Prophet, peace be upon him (pbuh), nazaare paak may be referring to his blessed look and stars to the 4 caliphs as well as all the other illustrious personalities (“My companions are like the stars.” ~ Prophet Muhammad, pbuh)

4 The poet expresses a wish to be in the lap of Baqi. Baqi may have several meanings here.
Jannatul Baqi is a blessed cemetery in Madina in which are found the holy shrines of the loved ones (families and companions) of the Prophet, pbuh. Al Baqi is also a Name of Allah, The One Who Remains. Thus, the lap here could imply to be drenched in the energy of Mercy of Allah among those beautiful souls.

5 The most beloved to Allah is none other than Huzoor Risaalat Ma’ab (pbuh) and the poet wishes for salaam [peace] to be upon those whom the Prophet (pbuh) loves. This hints allegorically to the Ahlul Bayt [People of the House] or special companions or the entire ummah [community] of the Prophet (pbuh). It is up to the reader to deduce further...

6 Hazrat Bilal, peace be upon him, used to give azan [call for prayer] in Masjid Nabawi (Prophet’s mosque) in the time of the Prophet, pbuh. His love for the Prophet, pbuh, is a lesson for humanity.

Commentaries

Heavy use of visual imagery and metaphor by the poet who is enchanted about every little aspect of Madina by virtue of its symbolism to Love of the Habib [Beloved] Prophet (SAW). 

The poet, through a lens of love, lovingly describes the weather, minarets, Jannatul Baqi, Khulafa-e Rashidin [the 4 mature caliphs] and even the intoxicated eyes of those who have the opportunity to gaze upon the Roza Mubarak [blessed shrine of the Prophet, pbuh].

Overarching themes of radiance/luminosity/resplendence – all symbolic of the Habib (SAW) being the most Illustrious - Shamm’en - most brightly shining in the line of Prophethood.

The environs of Madina described with such tenderness/love and honour - a palpable sense of electrifying love which pervades the entire eulogy.

One gets the feeling that the poet is almost compelled to share his love for the beloved of Allah (SAW) with us, through his words, while he is overwhelmed and almost strangled by love. Every line sears our hearts.

Through his words, we are privileged to enjoy a spiritual union with our Master (SAW).

Underlying all of this, one feels the pain in the heart of the poet, the pain of the genuine lover, persevering through the misery of separation.

Almost without exception, 'sufi poets of love' express their yearning, their deep sense of longing for an ideal that is embedded in their hearts, more beautiful than any image the mind can conjure up. The tangible reverence in the words and imagery enhances our capacity to absorb the meaning, with love and tenderness.

A storm stirs in the heart each time we hear these words. A storm of love!
